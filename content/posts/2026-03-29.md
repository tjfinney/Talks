## This is the Day

Psalm 118 is a processional hymn for the [Feast of Tabernacles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sukkot), when the Hebrews remember God's provision during their Exodus out of Egypt. Like many Psalms, this one has a *call and response* structure. According to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Call_and_response_(music)),

> In music, call and response is a compositional technique, often a succession of two distinct phrases that works like a conversation in music. One musician offers a phrase, and a second player answers with a direct commentary or response.

Let's try it out. One side will be the Dockers and the other side the Eagles:

> Dockers: Open the gates of justice!

> Eagles: I will enter and tell the LORD how thankful I am.

> Dockers: Here is the gate of the LORD!

> Eagles: Everyone who does right may enter this gate.

> Dockers: I praise the LORD for answering my prayers and saving me.

> Eagles: The stone that the builders tossed aside has now become the most important stone. 

> Everyone: The LORD has done this, and it is amazing to us.

> Dockers: This is the day that the LORD has made!

> Eagles: We will rejoice and be glad in it.

> Dockers: We'll ask the LORD to save us!

> Eagles: We'll sincerely ask the LORD to let us win.

> Dockers: God bless the one who comes in the name of the LORD!

> Eagles: We praise you from here in the house of the LORD.

> Everyone: The LORD is our God and he has given us light! 

> Dockers: Start the celebration!

> Eagles: March with palm branches all the way to the altar.

> Dockers: The LORD is my God!

> Eagles: I will praise him and tell him how thankful I am.

> Dockers: Tell the LORD how thankful you are,

> Eagles: because he is kind and always merciful.

[Psalm 118.19-29 CEV (adapted)]

The Festival of Booths happens in the Northern Hemisphere's autumn around September or October. Jesus rode into Jerusalem just before The Festival of Passover, which happens in the Northern Hemisphere's spring, around about now. Even though it wasn't the right time to start getting out the palm branches, the people did anyway because here was their King, riding on a donkey. This was the Day that the Lord had made, so they began to rejoice and be glad in it.

Reverend [Les Garrett](https://hymnary.org/person/Garrett_L) was born in New Zealand but lived and served for a long time in Perth and also lived in Narrogin. He took some of the words we just said and mixed them with a Fijian folk tune to produce a very famous chorus. Would the musicians be kind enough to help us sing it? We'll use the same call and response routine but this time with a repeated call and repeated response:

> Dockers: This is the day, this is the day

> Eagles: that the Lord has made, that the Lord has made

> Dockers: We will rejoice, we will rejoice

> Eagles: and be glad in it, and be glad in it

> Dockers: This is the day that the Lord has made

> Eagles: We will rejoice and be glad in it

> Everyone: This is the day, this is the day that the Lord has made.

Well, thank you everyone for taking part in that excellent rendition. I promise not to do any more call and response exercises in this sermon.

## All the way to the altar

There is much in Psalm 118 that we can appreciate in the light of the Gospel. It speaks of the gate of the Lord, the stone the builders rejected becoming the cornerstone, that it is God's doing, that this is the Day that the Lord has made. In the Gospel, Jesus says, *I am the gate* ([John 10.9](https://www.crosswire.org/study/parallelstudy.jsp?key=John+10%3A9#cv)). In the parable of the tenants, Jesus quotes these words about the stone that the builders rejected becoming the cornerstone and that it is the Lord's doing ([Matt 21.42](https://www.crosswire.org/study/parallelstudy.jsp?key=Matthew+21%3A42#cv)).

In the Psalm, the people shout out with joy and happiness saying, *Lord save us* and *We bless the One who comes in the name of the Lord.* They proclaim that God has given us light. All this resonates with the Gospel's teaching about Jesus, the Christ, the King, the Messiah. These parts of the psalm are a concise summary of his significance. There is also the somewhat mystifying line about marching with palm branches all the way to the altar...

## The Messiah enters Jerusalem

Matthew's account of the entry into Jerusalem begins with Jesus sending two of his disciples to get a colt, the foal of a donkey. He gives them these instructions:

> Go to the village facing you, and you will immediately find a tethered donkey and a colt with her. Untie them and bring them to me. If anyone says anything to you, you are to say, "The Master needs them and will send them back directly."

[[Matt 21.2-3 JB](https://www.crosswire.org/study/parallelstudy.jsp?key=Matthew+21%3A2#cv)]

If this were to happen today, it would be like someone saying, *Go to that place over there and once you get there you'll find a brand new motorbike tied up. Untie it and bring it back. If anyone asks what you're doing, say "The Boss needs it."*

How do you think that would go? It might raise questions about property and theft. You might not want to write down the names of the people who did it, and if I'm not wrong, none of the Gospels gives the two disciples' names. By the way, the Greek word here translated as *Master* or *Boss* is κυριοσ. It can mean *owner*, *Master*,  or *Lord* (as in *God*).

Anyway, that's what they did and they came back with the colt--one that had never been ridden--and his mother. They put cloaks on them and Jesus got on. Now Matthew is often careful to point out fulfillment of scripture in his Gospel, and true to form he does so here by quoting from the Prophet Zechariah:

> Say to the daughter of Zion:

> Look, your king comes to you;

> he is humble, he rides on a donkey

> and on a colt, the foal of a beast of burden.

[[Matt 21.5 JB, quoting Zech 9.9](https://www.crosswire.org/study/parallelstudy.jsp?key=Matthew+21%3A5#cv)]

A footnote in my Jerusalem Bible says at this point,

> In thus describing the messianic king's humble mount the prophet has in mind the unpretentious, unwarlike nature of his rule. Jesus, by performing this action, deliberately took to himself both the words of the prophecy and their deeper meaning.

[Jerusalem Bible, Matt 21.5, footnote a.]

One might say, *What makes them think that reading is about Jesus being "unwarlike"?* Digging into the context shows that it is reasonable to think so. Firstly there is the quote from Zechariah. Jesus is humble and rides on a donkey rather than a war horse. (Have you ever noticed how dictators tend to prance about on war horses? Just Google *Dictator on a horse* and see what images pop up.) Secondly there is the verse from Zechariah that follows the one Matthew quotes:

> He will banish chariots from Ephraim and horses from Jerusalem;

> the bow of war will be banished.

> He will proclaim peace for the nations.

> His empire shall stretch from sea to sea, from the River to the ends of the earth.

[Zech 9.10 JB](https://www.crosswire.org/study/parallelstudy.jsp?key=Zechariah+9%3A10#cv)

There is another piece of context as well, but it is warlike, not unwarlike. That context is the [Maccabean revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maccabean_Revolt), which was a Jewish uprising that had taken place many years before to drive foreigners out of Jerusalem. According to Wikipedia it was: 

> a Jewish rebellion led by the Maccabees against the Seleucid Empire and against Hellenistic influence on Jewish life... In 164 BCE, the Maccabees captured Jerusalem, a significant early victory. The subsequent cleansing of the temple and re-dedication of the altar on 25 Kislev is the source of the festival of Hanukkah.

Here is a reading from a book called *Second Maccabees*:

> They kept eight festal days with rejoicing, in the manner of the feast of Tabernacles, remembering how, not long before at the time of the feast of Tabernacles, they had been living in the mountains and caverns like wild beasts. Then, carrying branches, leafy boughs and palms, they offered hymns to him who had brought the cleansing of his own Holy Place to a happy outcome. They also decreed by public edict, ratified by vote, that the whole Jewish nation should celebrate those same days every year.

[2 Macc 10.6-8 JB]

Second Maccabees is one of the *deutero-canonical* books. It is not in the Hebrew Bible but is in the Septuagint, which is a Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible completed in the second century before Christ.

Given the connection between carrying palm branches and the battle against foreign occupation seen in Second Maccabees, some of the crowd waving palm branches when Jesus entered Jerusalem might have been thinking that he was the king who would liberate them from foreign occupation. However, Jesus is not that kind of king. He will banish the bow of war.

Somehow people always manage to get things wrong, particularly when it comes to Jesus the King. There is a terrible pattern. When Jesus is acknowledged as King, innocent blood is shed. The first time was when the Magi asked Herod, *Where is the infant king of the Jews? We saw his star as it rose and have come to do him homage.* What was Herod's response? He massacred all of the male babies aged two years or younger in and around Bethlehem. Their innocent blood was shed. The second time was a week after Jesus entered Jerusalem on the donkey to loud acclamation and waving palm branches. This time the Roman procurator, Pontius Pilate, condemned him to death at the insistence of the religious authorities and the crowd. The soldiers put in charge of his torture and execution dressed him as a king then mocked him, saying *Hail, King of the Jews.* Then they crucified him under a placard written by order of Pilate saying *King of the Jews*. His innocent blood was shed.

What hope is there for humanity, given that we have a knack for taking the things meant for life and turning them into death? The crowd celebrated Jesus' entry then a week later the crowd called for *his* death instead of a thug named Barabbas. Who can set us free from this deadly cycle of violence? We are hopeless cases... except for one thing. The events of Easter are God's doing. This is indeed the Day that the Lord has made. The march with palm branches goes all the way to the altar. At that altar God incarnate sacrifices himself to bring about reconciliation. The way is now open, we can go through the gate of justice and be saved. The gate is Jesus himself. He is the way. By entering through him we can start doing right instead of wrong, choosing life instead of death, making peace instead of war.

Amen
